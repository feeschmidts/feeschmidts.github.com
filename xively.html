Xively

Xively, formerly Pachube and Cosm, offers a platform, tools and services focused on simplifying and accelerating the creation of compelling products and solutions on the Internet of Things. I joined Cosm in November 2012 to help brand and design Xively ensuring a user centred design process with regular user testing. Since launch I'm working on new features, A/B test and refine the Xively development experience working closely with product owners and developers.

Retrospective

Retrospective

Retrospectives are part of the Scrum toolset. Every two weeks all Xively employees in London and Boston meet to discuss the processes and work of the last two weeks. The sessions are always planned around a 'game' which provide different nudges for positive and negative comments. The two games we play regularly are: 'Timeline' and 'Keep, Stop, Start'.

In Timeline, a horizontal line is drawn representing now until two weeks ago. Everyone writes events and things that happened during that period down onto post-its and sticks them into the timeline. Negative events are placed below the line, positive ones above. The difference between the line and the post-it represents the strength of the emotion.

Stop, Keep, Start - askes people to think of aspects or behaviours that should be 'stopped', 'started' or 'kept'.

With both games, whoever facilitates the session, reads out each post-it and the team has an opportunity to discuss. Action points are noted down and shared with the whole team. At the start of the next retro these action points get reviewed.

A/B Testing with Optimizely

A/B Testing

A/B testing with Xively's visitor rate let's one determine a relatively statistically relevant winner variation within a short amount of time. We often let experiments run between one and three weeks. We've utilised Optimizely to test a variety of button labels and form fields to increase inquires and signups.

Demonstrating benefits for frictioness forms

Sometimes Marketing requirements can be conflicting with User Experience, Optimizely allowed to gain evidence to remove form fields which sole purpose was 'collect everything possible from people to maybe utilize later for marketing'.

Improving flows to drive inquiries

For a long time, the Signup form didn't specify what type of account people are signing up for and didn't encourage finding out about pricing or commercial services. By adding a link and a small description up top, we increased commercial inquiries by 30%.

Things I've learned

Designing the Scrum way

At Xively we work in two week sprints. Every two weeks user stories are specified with criteria, prioritised, estimated and planned for the Sprint coming up. During the sprint we work on tasks to complete all planned user stories within the two weeks. After each sprint we have a review, where someone goes through the design and developer backlog and demonstrates all completes stories. I loved this approach as it kept us focused on what we're doing now, rather than getting distracted by the 'if then, what...'.

How can you know how long it will take you to research X? For any research related tasks, we normally applied a time box, and split the research and outcomes into chunks, for example for the navigation 'based on research - design a short term solution'.

How does it work with unplannable work or things that come up and need doing before the next sprint? How often is design work on website/ branding and sales collateral unplannable? If you ask people who don't plan, their answer is probably all the time! If there is a plan milestones can be set. These milestones translate into work chuncks specified as user stories, which then can be planned in depending on...

How to integrate the design backlog with the developer backlog? Reading any agile process description, they all state that teams should be interdisciplinary teams, together working on tasks. At Xively we never really achieved that... maybe because developers were very much backend and design needed to always keep ahead to finish things as quickly as possible.
